# Case study structure and best practices

Grounded in what hiring managers and recruiters consistently report looking for (UX Design Institute, IxDF, UXfolio, analyses of 60+ portfolios from hires at Google, Meta, Amazon, Apple).

## What reviewers actually do

- They spend **under 60 seconds** on the first scan. If the hook isn't there, they leave.
- They **scan, they don't read**. Structure must support finding role, problem, and impact without reading paragraphs.
- They read case studies to answer one meta-question: "What would I thank this person for 12 months from now?" (Jared Spool's hiring question). Judgment and impact answer that; polished UI alone does not.
- **2-4 deep case studies beat 6+ shallow ones.** Never include mediocre work to pad the count.
- A case study should be fully readable in about 5 minutes, scannable in 2.

## The structure

Use this section order. Headings can be phrased naturally per project, but the flow stays.

### 1. Hero / summary block (the 10-second version)
- Project title, plain language, no jargon
- One-line description of what it is
- Metadata row: client (or "personal project" / "concept"), role, timeframe, key tech/skills
- **The headline result, stated up front.** Example: "Rebuilt the site from scratch; load time dropped from 4.2s to 0.9s and the client's contact inquiries doubled." Reviewers who see the payoff first are far more willing to read how you got there.
- One strong hero image

### 2. Context and problem
- Who the client is and what they needed, in 2-4 sentences
- Why the problem mattered: the business/user pain, not just "they wanted a new website"
- Constraints: budget, timeline, tech, existing brand, stakeholders. Constraints make the later decisions meaningful.

### 3. My role
- Unambiguous. What Herman specifically owned, end to end or as part of a team.
- For solo client work: say so explicitly ("designed and built everything from concept to launch, solo"). This is a strength; state it plainly.

### 4. Process and key decisions (the heart of the case study)
This is the section most portfolios undercook and the one that gets people hired. For each major phase or decision:
- What the options/trade-offs were
- What was chosen and **why**
- Evidence of process: sketches, wireframes, research notes, iterations. Reviewers explicitly value seeing messy process artifacts as proof of real work.

Include at least one moment of friction: something that didn't work, client feedback that changed direction, a technical wall. Show the course-correction. This signals maturity, not weakness.

### 5. The solution
- Curated final screens/features, each with a caption explaining the thinking behind it
- Do NOT dump every screen. Select what carries the story.
- Link to the live site/prototype where possible.
- This is where interactive components (before/after slider etc.) earn their place.

### 6. Results and impact
- Quantitative where possible: performance metrics, Lighthouse, traffic, conversions, accessibility issues resolved, time/cost saved
- Qualitative where not: client quote, user feedback, what changed in practice
- For learning projects: validated learning counts as impact

### 7. Reflection
- 2-4 sentences: what would be done differently, what was learned
- Honest, specific, brief. "I'd involve the client in content earlier; late copy changes cost us a week" beats "I learned a lot about communication."

## Writing rules

- **Decisions over deliverables.** Every "what" needs a "why".
- **Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences).** Walls of text are the most cited case study killer.
- **Visuals lead, text supports.** Reviewers are often designers themselves.
- **Plain language.** No buzzwords, no inflated claims. Concrete details create credibility; superlatives destroy it.
- **Consistent layout across case studies** makes the whole portfolio feel cohesive and professional.
- **Own the AI-assisted workflow honestly.** Herman positions AI-assisted development as a differentiator. If AI tooling was part of the process, describe it the way any tool is described: what it was used for and how the output was validated. Never let a case study read as AI-generated filler; every claim must be explainable in an interview.
- Never use em dashes.

## Common failure modes to avoid

1. Screens without explanation (the #1 complaint)
2. Results buried at the bottom or missing entirely
3. Unclear personal contribution ("we did..." with no "I")
4. No friction, no trade-offs: reads as fabricated
5. Too long, covering every detail of the project instead of one strong thread
6. Jargon in titles and openings
7. Low-res or inconsistent imagery
8. Text that is obviously templated/AI-padded and can't be defended in an interview
